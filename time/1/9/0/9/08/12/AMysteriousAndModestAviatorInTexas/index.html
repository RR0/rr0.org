A Mysterious And Modest Aviator In Texas

Daily News de Galveston (Texas), 12 août 1909 Clark, J. E.: "airship, Texas, 1909", Magonia Exchange, 7 août 2007

Home | Traduction française

A mysterious airship, a kind of tramp craft of the sky, is prowling over Texas, if we may credit the reports of veracious witnesses in several sections of the state somewhat removed. It seems to have been seen first in Central Texas and last – that is, up to the time of this writing – in East Texas. In Central Texas it carried "three or four passengers,” but in East Texas not even one could be descried. However, as it flew over East Texas it was burning a headlight of dazzling brilliance which blinded the observers, so that one's first impulse was to fear that the passengers had fallen overboard between these two sections is dispelled. There is also considerable reassurance in the circumstances that there have been no reports of the finding of crushed and mangled bodies.

When [Edgar Allan] Poe imposed his balloon hoax on the public the balloon was seen nightly – never in daytime – until Poe himself was moved to confess that his story was made of nothing more substantial than the filaments of his own fertile fancy. Readers of Texas newspapers will recall the case of the belled buzzard, which is an instance proving the power of the imagination quite as well as did Poe's balloon hoax. This tintinabulating buzzard was descried first in Northeast Texas, near Paris, if memory is not at fault; thence he floated leisurely down into Bell County, his course being closely marked every day by a string of circumstantial reports based on the testimony of men whose names, residences and occupations were given. We recall distinctly that a number of ministers were among those who testified to having seen the belled buzzard, the particular significance of which circumstance we need not point out. But the belled buzzard disappeared, and, what is more, in a section of the state where news gathering facilities are excellent.

For which, and other reasons, we are quite sure these folk think they have seen a mysterious airship floating over East Texas, and, of course, we are equally sure they didn't see one. They looked into a midsummer night's sky and saw a picture their imaginations limned on that starlit and canopied canvas. They, and all of us, see many other things that are quite as vivid and realistic – and quite as unsubstantial. Fancy plays us those tricks. Sometimes they are kind and sometimes they are unkind. It's a matter – the tricks themselves – of psychology, in which, indeed, there is more of law worth knowing than most of us dream of.
