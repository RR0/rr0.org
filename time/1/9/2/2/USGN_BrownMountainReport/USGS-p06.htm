Ceux qui ont vu les lumières depuis le sud ou l'est pourraient à juste titre contester qu'aucun phare de locomotive ne peut être vu au nord et au nord-ouest. Une bonne carte topographique, cependant, montre de nombreuses routes sur lesquelles un phare d'automobile pourrait intervenir entre un observateur et Brown Mountain de manière à donner pour la plupart le même effet que celui que l'on obtiendrait en le regardant par-dessus la montagne depuis Loven's ou Blowing Rock.

Il existe 2 bâtiments au sommet de Brown Mountain. Un est la propriété du Club de Brown Mountain et l'autre est une station du Service des Forêts. Les lumières de ces derniers et les feux de campeurs sont les seules lumières connues provenir de Brown Mountain et les seules qui aient été vues sur la montagne par des observateurs sur la montagne. Il existe également des bâtiments sur les southern spurs of Brown and Adams Mountains. Seen from favorable viewpoints these might furnish lights that could be interpreted as manifestations of the Brown Mountain light.

For some years there have been lumber camps in Upper Creek west of Brown Mountain. Some of the buildings at these camps are on the sides of the valley or on Ripshin Ridge. Viewed from favorable positions lights in these buildings might be ascribed to the Brown Mountain light. Rare electrical discharges similar to those reported from western Virginia may take place on Brown Mountain or on other ridges on the Blue Ridge front, but they could have nothing in common with the ordinary Brown Mountain light either in appearance or in regularity of occurrence.

The lights seen by Mr. Martin from Adams Mountain can probably not be satisfactorily explained after so long a lapse of time. There is no reason to attribute to them supernatural or unusual origin, and they cannot be explained as due to mirage, which is Mr. Martin's idea. The suggestion that they might have been caused by moonshiners carrying lanterns has been rejected because of the roughness of the east side of Brown Mountain and because of the distance that the lights seemed to travel in 20 minutes. They might be due to fireflies flying relatively near Mr. Martin yet appearing unduly large because his eyes were focused on the distant hillside, the appearance of going in and out of ravines being due to intermittence in the lights, but H. S. Barber, an entomologist of the Division of Insects of the National Museum, to whom the matter was referred, states that this explanation, though possible, is improbable, chiefly because of the lateness of the hour of observation.
