RD B.: ""Ghosts are Marsh Gases" (1925)", Magonia Exchange, 2007-08-02

"Walking ghosts" seen recently in the Hurley district of South Dakota, which greatly alarmed and mystified the xxxxers of the district, and "balls of fire" reported floating at night over the northern prairies of Nebraska, and in various parts of South Dakota, have been explained by Freeman Ward, state geologist and professor at the University of South Dakota.

These strange lights and ghostly figures in the opinion of Professor Ward, really are nothing more than bubbles of marsh gas oozing out from the swamp land and catching fire as they emerge. Professor Ward points out they are commonly known as the "Will o' the Wisp," a phenomenon that has frequently been seen in nearly every part of the world. Some scientists believe this phenomenon is of a radio active or electrical character. Professor Ward maintains that before Nebraska and the Dakotas, the Indians it is said, feared and worshiped these mysterious lights. The story is told of an Indian brave, who, terrorized by an approaching ball of fire, became crazed and thereafter roamed the prairies a screaming maniac. Indians among whom the legend is current believe that these mysterious lights are shadows of departed red men, whose wrathful spirits come back in this flaming form to wreak vengeance upon the "paleface" and to spread terror over their once "happy hunting ground."

For several weeks past South Dakota folks have been told a story about a "walking ghost," which was supposed to haunt a house and old well on a farm neary Hurley, S. D.
