RD B.: ""Ghosts are Marsh Gases" (1925)", Magonia Exchange, 2007-08-02

Les "fantômes en marche" vu récemment dans le quartier de Hurley dans le Dakota du sud, qui alarmèrent grandement et mystifièrent les xxxxers du quartier, et les "boules de feu" signalées flottant de nuit au-dessus des prairies du nord du Nebraska, et dans diverses parties du Dakota du sud, ont été expliqués par Freeman Ward, géologue d'état et professeur à l'Université du Dakota du sud.

Ces lumières étranges et figures fantômatiques ne sont, de l'opinion du professeur Ward, vraiment rien d'autre que des bulles de gaz de marais oozing out from the swamp land and catching fire as they emerge. Professor Ward points out they are commonly known as the "Will o' the Wisp," a phenomenon that has frequently been seen in nearly every part of the world. Some scientists believe this phenomenon is of a radio active or electrical character. Professor Ward maintains that before Nebraska and the Dakotas, the Indians it is said, feared and worshiped these mysterious lights. The story is told of an Indian brave, who, terrorized by an approaching ball of fire, became crazed and thereafter roamed the prairies a screaming maniac. Indians among whom the legend is current believe that these mysterious lights are shadows of departed red men, whose wrathful spirits come back in this flaming form to wreak vengeance upon the "paleface" and to spread terror over their once "happy hunting ground."

For several weeks past South Dakota folks have been told a story about a "walking ghost," which was supposed to haunt a house and old well on a farm neary Hurley, S. D.
